# LinkedIn Posts

> **The first 140 characters are everything.** LinkedIn truncates behind "see more." If they don't click, nothing else matters. For hook formulas, see [headlines.md](headlines.md).

## Algorithm Signals That Matter (2026)

LinkedIn's algorithm ranks posts by engagement quality, not vanity metrics. The three primary signals:

| Signal | Weight | How to Trigger It |
|--------|--------|-------------------|
| Dwell time (61+ seconds) | Primary relevance signal | Short paragraphs, progressive revelation, one insight per paragraph |
| Comments | 15x the value of likes | Specific, debatable closing question that experts feel compelled to answer |
| Saves/Bookmarks | Strong quality signal | Reference-quality analysis with specific data |
| Shares with commentary | Extended network reach | Quotable bold-text lines worth adding commentary to |
| Topic authority match | Off-network distribution | Consistent niche posting builds "topic DNA" for your profile |

**Key benchmarks:**
- Posts with 61+ seconds dwell time: **15.6% engagement rate**
- Posts with <3 seconds dwell time: **1.2% engagement rate**
- The 15-second threshold triggers a **reach multiplier**
- First 60 minutes are critical: only 5% of underperforming posts recover

## The External Link Penalty

Posts with external links get approximately **60% less reach**. This is why the pipeline puts links in the **first comment**, not the post body.

The first comment should contain:
```
Read more in Hyperscale News: [URL]
```

Never put URLs in the post text itself.

## Dwell Time Structure (Most Important Concept)

Dwell time is how long a reader spends on your post. It's LinkedIn's primary quality signal because 90% of users consume content without interacting (liking, commenting). Dwell time captures that silent majority.

### How to Structure for Dwell Time

Build every post as a **chain of short paragraphs** where each one raises a question the next one answers:

1. **First line (the hook):** A provocative claim under 140 characters that creates a gap
2. **Second paragraph:** A specific data point that deepens the gap
3. **Third paragraph:** A new tension or consequence the reader didn't expect
4. **Fourth paragraph:** The insight or synthesis that ties it together
5. **Closing question:** A specific, debatable question that triggers comments

The reader should feel **incomplete after every paragraph**. If they can stop reading after paragraph 2 and feel satisfied, the structure is wrong.

### What Kills Dwell Time

- Dense paragraphs with 3+ data points crammed together (readers skim, don't read)
- "List then conclude" structure (readers jump to the conclusion and leave)
- Front-loading all the interesting data (nothing left to discover)

## Hook Rules (First 140 Characters)

The hook must pass two tests:
1. **Under 140 characters** (before LinkedIn truncates)
2. **Standalone compelling claim** (if someone sees only this line, they click "see more")

### Hooks That Work

| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Consequence without cause | "6.5 GW under construction and 30% of it won't land on time." |
| Contrarian take | "The developers who'll win in Texas aren't the ones with the most megawatts." |
| Surprising comparison | "ERCOT took 55 years to build 80 GW. Datacenter developers matched half that in two." |
| Bold synthesis | "Texas isn't losing the datacenter race. It's winning so fast the infrastructure can't keep up." |

### Hooks That Fail

| Pattern | Example | Why It Fails |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Data dump first | "Project A. Project B. Project C." | Punchline buried under evidence |
| Setup sentence | "Here's what you need to know about..." | Throat-clearing, not a claim |
| Generic framing | "The infrastructure landscape is shifting." | AI-sounding, no specificity |

## Discussion Questions (Comment Driver)

Comments are **15x more valuable** than likes. Every post MUST end with a specific, debatable question before the hashtags.

**Works:**
- "Should ERCOT require water disclosure before interconnection approval, or does that create a permitting bottleneck?"
- "Is captive generation a permanent feature of datacenter power, or a transitional phase until grid capacity catches up?"
- "Where's the line between 'water-conscious design' and 'we added a recycling bullet point to the permit application'?"

**Fails:**
- "What do you think?" (generic, nobody feels compelled)
- "What are you seeing in your market?" (too broad, low commitment to answer)
- Any statement, no matter how strong, that isn't a question
- "Excited to hear your thoughts" (LinkedIn platitude)

## Post Formats

### Text Post (POST 1)
- 150-225 words (900-1,350 characters)
- Short paragraphs (1-2 sentences each)
- Bold emphasis paragraphs for standalone assertions
- Ends with discussion question
- 3-5 hashtags on the final line
- No URLs in the post body

### Carousel-Ready Post (POST 2)
- Same word count and structure rules as text post
- Content structured as 5-8 discrete progression points
- Each point is a self-contained insight backed by one data point
- Includes a CAROUSEL SLIDES section with slide descriptions
- The post text works standalone even without the carousel

## Topic Authority

LinkedIn's algorithm builds a "topic DNA" for each creator and distributes content to interested users **outside your network**. Consistency in niche topics builds reach faster than variety.

Hyperscale News's topic authority domains:
- Texas datacenter infrastructure
- ERCOT grid planning and interconnection
- Datacenter water resources and permitting
- Texas energy policy and regulation
- Datacenter power procurement and behind-the-meter generation

Avoid generic "AI infrastructure" or "tech industry" framing. The more specific the topic, the better LinkedIn's interest graph serves it.

## What NOT to Do

- **Put links in the post body** (60% reach penalty; use first comment)
- **Use "shadow grid"** (implies interconnection where none exists)
- **Write 4+ sentence paragraphs** (dwell time killer on mobile)
- **End with a statement instead of a question** (wastes the comment opportunity)
- **Use "today's" references** (posts may be reused on different days)
- **Throat-clear** ("Here's the tension," "Here's what you need to know")
- **Hedge** ("it remains to be seen," "arguably," "only time will tell")
- **Repeat themes within 7 days** (the pipeline tracks recent themes and blocks repeats)
- **Use unprovable superlatives** when concrete comparisons exist
- **Generic discussion questions** ("What do you think?" "Thoughts?")
