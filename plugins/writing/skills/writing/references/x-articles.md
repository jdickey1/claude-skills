# X Articles (Long-Form)

> **Title + first 4 lines are everything.** The reader sees ONLY the title and opening before the "Show more" fold. If they don't expand, nothing else matters. For hook formulas and testing methodology, see [headlines.md](headlines.md).

## Why Native Articles Beat Link Posts

X's algorithm treats Articles as native content. No link penalty. Higher dwell time (users stay on-platform). Permanent placement on your profile. Full formatting: headers, bold, embedded media.

Dan Koe's X Article hit 150M+ views with 900K followers. The algorithm artificially boosts Article distribution because X needs adoption of the format. This is a temporary arbitrage window.

**Key data:** Non-Premium accounts posting external links get zero median engagement (March 2025 onwards). Native Articles bypass that entirely.

## Algorithm Signals That Matter

X Articles inherit the same engagement weights as posts, but dwell time and scroll depth add additional signals:

| Signal | Weight | How Articles Trigger It |
|--------|--------|------------------------|
| Reply to discussion question | **27x** (150x if author replies back) | Specific, debatable closing question |
| Bookmark | **10x** | Reference-quality analysis with specific data |
| Quote tweet | **25x** | Quotable bold-text lines worth adding commentary to |
| Profile click | **12x** | Consistent expertise, unique perspective |
| Dwell time | Bonus multiplier | Short paragraphs + whitespace = readers scroll through |
| Expand click | Feed signal | Title + opening must create genuine curiosity gap |

**The single most valuable structural choice:** End every article with a specific, debatable question that industry experts feel personally compelled to answer. Then reply to the replies. That interaction loop is worth 150 likes per reply chain.

## The Curiosity Gap (Most Important Concept)

The title + first 2-3 sentences must create a gap between what the reader knows and what they need to find out. The reader should feel INCOMPLETE after reading only the title.

### Titles That Work

| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Consequence without cause | "Texas Has Two Power Systems Now. One of Them Didn't Exist Two Years Ago." |
| Tension / contradiction | "47 Power Plants Are Going Up Across America. None of Them Connect to the Grid." |
| Absurdity without resolution | "A Single Customer Is Getting a Dedicated 2,200 MW Power Plant. The Neighbors Have Questions." |
| Hidden variable | "Every Texas Datacenter Developer Knows Their Power Plan. Almost None Know This." |

### Titles That Fail

| Pattern | Example | Why It Fails |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| States the conclusion | "Water Will Decide Which Texas Data Centers Actually Get Built" | Reader already has the punchline |
| Generic problem + condescension | "Texas's 6.5 GW Boom Has a Water Problem Nobody's Solving" | "Nobody's" talks down to readers who ARE tracking it |
| Labels the phenomenon | "Off-Grid Datacenters Are Building a Shadow Power System" | Conclusion stated + banned "shadow" metaphor |
| Thesis as title | "Big Tech's Shadow Grid Is Everyone's Problem" | No gap. Just a position statement. |

### Opening Paragraph

The first 2-3 sentences DEEPEN the gap, they don't resolve it. Use a concrete comparison that feels almost unbelievable:

**Good:** "ERCOT has had 55 years to build 80 GW of dispatchable power. In just two years, datacenter developers have built or contracted half that amount."

**Bad:** "The hyperscale industry is quietly building a parallel energy system, and the 150 million Americans who depend on the existing grid are going to pay for it." (This is a thesis statement. It resolves the title instead of deepening curiosity.)

## Structure Rules

### Paragraph Length: 1-3 Sentences MAX

This is the single biggest quality gap in pipeline-generated articles. On mobile X, dense paragraphs are unreadable gray slabs. The best-performing article in the archive has 1-2 sentence paragraphs with bold stat anchors throughout.

**The pipeline enforces this programmatically** by splitting paragraphs >3 sentences after generation. But write short from the start.

### Bold Stat Anchors

Scanners (most readers) grab bold text first. Every article needs 3-5 bold stats or findings as visual anchors:

> **40 GW of private generation capacity is coming regardless.** The only question is whether the regulatory framework is ready when it arrives.

### Section Headers

2-3 headers per article using `##` format. Headers must be:
- Specific and informative ("28 Generators, One Chokehold" not "Background")
- Never condescending ("The Scale Nobody Is Tracking" assumes your expert audience isn't tracking it)
- Named after the insight, not the topic

### Word Count: 500-800 Words

Hard limit. Anything over 800 gets flagged by the programmatic pre-check. The best-performing article in the archive is ~700 words. Tight analysis gets shared. Bloated analysis gets abandoned mid-scroll.

## Closing: Always a Discussion Question

Every article MUST end with a specific, debatable question. This is the #1 reply driver.

**Works:**
- "What's your read: is self-supplied datacenter power a permanent feature, or a transitional phase?"
- "Should ERCOT study the Entergy-Meta model as a framework for managing concentrated load, or does Texas's deregulated structure make it fundamentally inapplicable?"
- "Where's the line between 'water-conscious design' and 'we added a recycling bullet point to the permit application'?"

**Fails:**
- "What do you think?" (generic, nobody feels compelled)
- "If you're navigating water permitting, reach out." (LinkedIn CTA, not X-native, generates zero replies)
- Any statement, no matter how strong, that isn't a question

## What NOT to Do

- **End with "reach out"** or any CTA variant (LinkedIn language, kills reply signal)
- **Use "shadow grid"** (implies interconnection where none exists; use "private generation," "self-supplied power," "behind the meter")
- **Repeat the same theme** within a 7-day window (the pipeline tracks recent article themes and blocks repeats)
- **Write 4+ sentence paragraphs** (the pipeline splits these automatically, but it's better to write short)
- **Use unprovable superlatives** ("most consequential in history," "unprecedented") when concrete comparisons exist
- **Throat-clear** ("Here's the tension," "Consider what's happening," "The deeper question is whether")
- **Hedge** ("nobody disputes," "it remains to be seen," "arguably")
- **Talk down** in headers ("The Scale Nobody Is Tracking," "What Most People Miss")
- **Add a hero/header image** unless it adds genuine information (the best-performing article had NO image)

## Gold Standard: The Revised Feb 15 Article

"Texas Has Two Power Systems Now. One of Them Didn't Exist Two Years Ago."

What it does right:
- Title creates genuine curiosity gap (two power systems? what?)
- Opening deepens with the ERCOT comparison (55 years vs 2 years)
- Bold stat anchors throughout for scanners
- 1-2 sentence paragraphs with whitespace
- Ends with a specific debatable question, not "reach out"
- No hero image (pure text, clean on mobile)
- ~700 words (within target range)
- No "shadow grid," no throat-clearing, no hedging
